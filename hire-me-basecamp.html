I've experimented with some gluten free options here, and you can get okay results using a gluten free baking mix, but in order to truly nail this recipe, you'll have to embrace the gluten-y goodness.

Making the perfect pizza crust at home

I've made the distinction "at home" here, because as much as I'd like it, I just don't have an oven capable of producting temperatures of 700 to 900 degrees (like a commercial pizza oven). My oven maxes out at 500 degrees. That's good enough for our needs though. You will need one piece of specialized gear for this: you're going to need a baking stone/pizza stone. Go ahead and put that in your oven and turn it up to the highest temperature you can get before we get started.

The ingredient list:

  • 2 ½ cups all-purpose flour
  • ½ cup semolina (optional, but gives you a little extra texture)
  • 1 cup warm water
  • 2 tsp. active dry yeast
  • 1 tbsp. sugar
  • 1 tbsp. salt

Step 1: Mix the water, yeast and sugar together in a bowl and let the mixture sit. After a few minutes, it should start to get frothy/bubbly (indicating that the yeast is good).

Step 2: Measure the all-purpose flour and semolina flour into a mixing bowl. I'm lazy, so for me it goes into the bowl on my stand mixer and I attach the dough hook attachment. Add the salt (yes, a full tablespoon!)

Step 3: Start the mixer on low and start to slowly add the frothy yeast mixture from step 1.

Step 4: Once you've added all of your water, turn the speed up on the mixer a notch. Keep an eye on your dough and when it just starts to come together/not look completely dried out, stop the mixer.

Step 5: Sprinkle some flour on your counter/table/cutting board and turn the dough out on to the floured surface. Continue to work the dough by hand until it's elastic-y and stretches well.

Step 6: Divide your dough up. I'll usually divide this amount into 4 and make 4 smaller pizzas because that's easier to work with. You can go bigger if you want!

Step 7: Let your dough rise for a half hour or so. It should roughly double in size.

Step 8: Flour up a cutting board and turn one of your portions out. You can use a rolling pin if you like, but it's also just fine to work it with you hands until it's the right shape/size and thickness. I try for roundish and a 10 inch diameter (but as you can see in the picture, I'm not a perfectionist -- just call it "rustic"). The flour on your cutting board here prevents the dough from sticking because we're going to transfer it to your pizza stone in a second.

Step 9: Add some sauce, some toppings, some cheese. Live dangerously and throw some pineapple on there. Just don't tell Gordon Ramsay [nsfw].

Step 10: Now transfer that pie directly on to your pizza stone. Try to resist opening the oven door for the next 10 minutes. The pizza is done when the crust starts to brown up around the edges.

Remove it from the oven, let it cool for about 5 minutes and slice it up.

What makes this recipe better than others is a) the texture. Don't over-mix the dough and it'll be so stretchy/good to chew. And b) the salt. So many crusts are just flavorless. That's no fun at all.

Hi Time Tracker Tina,

We don't have time tracking built in with Basecamp 3. There are a few services out there that integrate with Basecamp and specialize in time tracking (Toggl and Harvest are a couple.)

Jon

Can I create recurring events in the Basecamp calendar? What about recurring todo items?

Hi Repeating Randy,

Thanks for using Basecamp.

You can set up recurring events in Basecamp's calendar. After you've clicked on "New Event", and entered the details of your event, click on "Don't repeat". From there, you can select one of the options available to have your event repeat on a schedule. We have more information about the schedule and the options available in our help section.

If we can help you out with anything else, don't hesitate to give us a shout.

Jon

Do you offer 2fa for signing in?

Hi Security Sally,

Thanks for getting in touch!

We have a couple of options for 2 factor authentication with Basecamp. You can either set up phone verification for your Basecamp account and we'll send you a text with a code when you sign in. Alternatively, if you/your company use Google Apps, you can enable signing in with Google on your account and Google has built-in 2 factor authentication.

There's more information about logging in to your account in our help section.

Let me know if you have any other questions.

Jon
