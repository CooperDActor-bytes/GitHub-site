Pick any toppings you like, as long as the crust is on point, you'll have a fantastic dish. I've experimented with some gluten free options here, and you can get okay results using a gluten free baking mix, but in order to truly nail this recipe, you'll have to embrace the gluten-y goodness.

Making the perfect pizza crust at home

I've made the distinction "at home" here, because as much as I'd like it, I just don't have an oven capable of producting temperatures of 700 to 900 degrees (like a comercial pizza oven). My oven maxes out at 500 degrees. That's good enough for our needs though. You will need one piece of specialized gear for this: you're going to need a baking stone/pizza stone. Go ahead and put that in your oven and turn it up to the highest temperature you can get before we get started.

The ingredient list:

  • 2 ½ cups all-purpose flour
  • ½ cup semolina (optional, but gives you a little extra texture)
  • 1 cup warm water
  • 2 tsp. active dry yeast
  • 1 tbsp. sugar
  • 1 tbsp. salt

Step 1: Mix the water, yeast and sugar together in a bowl and let the mixture sit. After a few minutes, it should start to get frothy/bubbly (indicating that the yeast is good).

Step 2: Measure the all-purpose flour and semolina flour into a mixing bowl. I'm lazy, so for me it goes into the bowl on my stand mixer and I attach the dough hook attachment. Add the salt (yes, a full tablespoon!)

Step 3: Start the mixer on low and start to slowly add the frothy yeast mixture from step 1.

Step 4: Once you've added all of your water, turn the speed up on the mixer a notch. Keep an eye on your dough and when it just starts to come together/not look completely dried out, stop the mixer.

Step 5: Sprinkle some flour on your counter/table/cutting board and turn the dough out on to the floured surface. Continue to work the dough by hand until it's elastic-y and stretches well.

Step 6: Divide your dough up. I'll usually divide this amount into 4 and make 4 smaller pizzas because that's easier to work with. You can go bigger if you want!

Step 7: Let your dough rise for a half hour or so. It should roughly double in size.

Step 8: Flour up a cutting board and turn one of your portions out. You can use a rolling pin if you like, but it's also just fine to work it with you hands until it's the right shape/size and thickness. I try for roundish and a 10" diameter.
